Oz!

Well, I like Oz but I like Tara. But which is better? Oz or Tara? There's only one way to find out...

Tara is the reason I love Buffy but that doesn't mean I can't like the character of Oz. He was so cool. And he had taste.


I've always liked this episode. I think Ampata's situation is heartbreaking. The urge to live again when your life had been curtailed unjustly is a powerful one.

I like the explanation for Ampata's good English. I also like my family's explanation for the appearance of a z-bed for Ampata in Buffy's room (but was Buffy really going to share her room with a boy?): although it is later shown that the Summers live in a three bedroomed house since Dawn has a room of her own, however, just like Dawn the bedroom doesn't exist yet so a z-bed is required in this reality's two bedroomed house.

  • Stephanie Beaver on 26 February 2011 said:
    I actually think that Willow's love life bears up well when scrutinized for hints that she might be gay. And Willow seemed to identify as a lesbian, not a bisexual woman, even though the writers never really wanted to go into the complexity of queer identity later in the show. The biggest hint comes in "Doppelgangland" when Willow observes that Vamp Willow is "so evil. And skanky. And I think I'm kinda gay." Buffy tries to assure Willow that the vampire versions of people do not resemble who they were in life, which causes Angel to say "Actually--" But then he stops himself and agrees with Buffy when he sees the girls' faces. At the beginning of the show and well through the third season, Willow crushes on Xander, whom she knows is not interested in her. It's actually not uncommon at all from accounts I've heard from various people for those who later come out as queer to have such a crush on opposite-sex best friends and other unattainable people. It allows people to, even unconsciously, perform the heterosexuality without having to engage in heterosexual romantic activities with which they might not feel too comfortable. Also, the physical side Oz and Willow's relationship progressed very slowly and was never a big part of their relationship, which was built more on mutual caring and affection. I'm not trying to say that straight couples cannot have relationships that look like Willow and Oz's, but even Joss cites the downplayed sexual aspect of their relationship as a retconned "hint" that Willow might be gay in one of the episode commentaries, I forget which one. Finally, just because a someone eventually realizes that they prefer being in same-sex relationships, it doesn't mean that they were necessarily unhappy in their opposite-sex relationships. Some people do articulate that they were never attracted to the opposite sex or that they "felt something was missing" in their heterosexual relationships. However, I'm currently lying next to a woman who was with a man for 12 years and never felt either of those things, but now very firmly identifies as a lesbian. It's fine if you "couldn't see" Willow as gay or didn't think there were enough hints that she was because there weren't. Willow was not conceived as a queer character. However, I think sexuality is always more complicated than we would prefer.
